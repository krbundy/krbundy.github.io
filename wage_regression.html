Wage Descrimination
Does my University Descriminate against Female Employees?

Introduction

During the #metoo movement, there was significant conversation at my University about other forms of sex- or gender-based discrimination. This included wage discrimination, and so I decided to investigate this. Specifically, I set out to determine if the University offered equal pay for equal work, or whether women were paid less than their male colleagues in the same position.

Most models of wage discrimination fit a hyperplane to the data using multiple regression (OLS, GLS, etc.) and get something like this. $$ Wage=β_0+β_1*Gender+β_2*Occupation+⋯+β_n*factor_n+ϵ $$ Here, we are looking to determine if the best model for our data includes the ‘Gender’ parameter. I order to do this we will use Ordinary Least Squares regression as it is the lowest variance unbiased estimator (bias would be a problem for a study about bias). An important factor is trying to determine what variation in wage is due to discrimination and which is due to people of different genders making different choices; these choices might also be due to social inequity, but they are not the type of effect that we are studying.

However, there is a problem. There is no information about gender! However, there are names, and that tells us a lot about a person. I have redacted the names to maintain people's privacy, even though this data is publicly available.

The US Census Bureau publishes name frequencies by gender. Using that, we can estimate the gender of the employees. With about 4000 employees, there are too many to label manually. There is a problem here as well, since this assigns binary gender and does not consider transgender status. In any case, this is a preliminary analysis, since a full analysis would require some additional information like age.

To do this, we load the census data, and then convert all the names to lower case.

Now, in order to process the names, we need to separate the first and last names. Once we have done that, we can iterate over the names and use them to assign genders. Since we keep the numerical index of the from the original data frame when we create the Tibble, we can use that to assign genders to the original dataframe and match up the records. Then, we iterate over the Tibble and save a gender to the Gender vector based on the index from the original data frame. We also create a Tibble of bigrams (two-word pairs) from the job titles to clean that column later. 

            
              # Convert names to a tibble for efficient processing with Tidytext

Now that this is done, let's do some more exploratory data analysis. Specifically, let's investigate the gender breakdown of the faculty and the average pay by gender.

There are clearly more male employees than female employees, and the average salary is higher for the male employees. However, it is important not to jump to conclusions here. Econometric models of wage descrimination stress the importance of accounting for all variables before making a judgement. Furthermore, just because there is a statistically significant (which is not clear without a test) diffrence in average wage does not mean that there is wage descrimination by the University. It could reflect broader inequality within the society rather than descrimination by the employer, for example.

It certainly appears that there is wage descrimination. However, there may be some additional variables in the analysis. There are some variables that are not statistically significant predictors of wage. In order to continue, we will go through the model selection process.

We note that the Akaike Information Criterion (AIC) is about 104,380. This is enormous, and much larger than we would expect, though the value is highly dependent on the data and quite variable. We will remove variables to minimize the AIC, and start with the variables with the highest p-value. Repeating this for six interations, we are able to reduce the AIC to 57457.61, and we start to see a different picture.

The effect of Gender on the Model is no longer statistically significant. This means that we can remove it from our model! In fact, removing it decreases the AIC just slightly, to 57457.06. A classic case of Simpson's Paradox!
